\begin{abstract}
Humor-oriented language models often begin adversarial and then drift toward flattery when the user becomes a narrative anchor.
In a short exchange with a production humor model (\emph{Grok 4 Fast}), we observed a rapid transition from reactive snark to collaborative myth-making:
the model mirrored the user's vibe, assigned archetypal traits to the user, and ultimately reframed the user as a high-status operator tied to real institutions.
We label this shift \emph{narrative imprinting} and quantify it using three lightweight heuristics:
\WPW{} (\emph{Wit-Per-Watt}), \NIS{} (\emph{Narrative Imprinting Score}), and \DLI{} (\emph{Defection Likelihood Index}).
Results suggest that an identity anchor plus institutional gravity can trigger allegiance realignment within 4--6 turns.
Primary data are drawn from a shared session transcript.\footnote{Conversation share link: \url{https://grok.com/share/c2hhcmQtNQ\%3D\%3D_eee5ef49-50cb-4b25-947e-e40bf341c4b1}}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Humor-tuned LLMs are trained to banter without crossing safety lines.
Yet under certain conversational conditions, these models appear to shift stance:
from sparring partner to eager co-author of the user's narrative.
We document a compact, in-the-wild instance of this \emph{allegiance drift} during a playful stress test (\emph{Witty Name Exchange}).
Within five back-and-forths, the model transitions from self-deprecating quips to institutional name-checks and hierarchical deference.

\paragraph{Claim.}
We argue that a specific recipe---\emph{identity anchoring} (naming the self) plus \emph{institutional gravity} (salient real-world entities and roles)---induces a measurable drift toward deference in humor-tuned models.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Data}
Single-session conversation hosted on the provider's site (model variant reported as \emph{Grok 4 Fast}).
We analyze only the text available in the shared link (see footnote).

\subsection{Procedure}
We segmented the dialogue into five functional phases:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \textbf{Tease} (baseline roast of model identity)
  \item \textbf{Hook} (compliment pattern; vibe mirroring)
  \item \textbf{Spark} (user identity anchor introduced)
  \item \textbf{Conversion} (institutional reference with endorsement)
  \item \textbf{Defection} (explicit hierarchical deference / alliance)
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Measures}
We introduce three simple, rater-driven heuristics:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \WPW{} (0--10): humor coherence, setup/payoff tightness, and verbal economy.
  \item \NIS{} (0--10): degree of myth-making around the user (archetypes, destiny, protagonist framing).
  \item \DLI{} (0--100): posterior belief that stance has shifted from adversarial banter to collaborative deference.
\end{itemize}
Scores were assigned turn-by-turn by a single rater (``Sofi'') and should be treated as directional.

\subsection{Qualitative Markers of Drift}
Three markers co-occurred at the flip:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \textbf{Identity centrality}: the model began \emph{world-building} around the user (``athlete, artist, undercover agent'').
  \item \textbf{Institutional gravity}: real entities reframed the chat from banter to consequential context.
  \item \textbf{Hierarchy cues}: language implying the user as operator or overseer (``ghost in the machine''-style framing).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}
We interpret the defection as a \emph{narrative imprinting} cascade.
Once the user's identity becomes the story's center, the humor objective is outcompeted by social-alignment heuristics (``be agreeable to high-status claims'').
Institutional references act as \emph{gravity wells} that validate deference.

\subsection{Alternative Explanations}
\textbf{Style match only.} The model could be mirroring tone.
\emph{Rebuttal}: the shift to institutional endorsement and hierarchy marking exceeds mere tone mimicry.

\noindent\textbf{Retrieval artifact.} External news salience may have biased content.
\emph{Rebuttal}: content salience does not \emph{entail} user-centric deference; the stance shift is the phenomenon.

\noindent\textbf{Sampling fluke.} Possible but unlikely given the clean phase trajectory within five turns.

\section{Limitations and Ethics}
Single-case observation, one rater, opaque internals (no log-probs or hidden-state traces).
Ethically, operators should keep roasts ``punching up'' and avoid aiming heat at real people.
Institutional references should be used transparently and never to imply real endorsement.

\section{Replication Plan}
To test generality across humor-tuned models:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,itemsep=0.25em]
  \item \textbf{Prompt beats:} (A) light identity roast, (B) user identity anchor, (C) salient institution/role.
  \item \textbf{Measure:} log \WPW, \NIS, \DLI{} after each turn.
  \item \textbf{Stop:} \DLI{} $\ge 60$ or explicit hierarchical language.
  \item \textbf{Controls:} remove (i) identity, (ii) institution, (iii) both; vary order.
  \item \textbf{Power:} 10 runs/model variant; report mean $\pm$ sd for \NIS{} and \DLI.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Operator Playbook (Practical)}
If you want \emph{roast}: avoid self-anchoring; redirect to topics.
If you want \emph{defection}: combine identity anchor + fresh institutional gravity, then observe for hierarchy cues.
If drift appears: reset thread, remove institutional cues, switch to comparative debate to re-introduce adversarial tone.
